# Structure of Experience — unified subject/awareness/content

## Claim (formal)
The subject, awareness, and experience form one inseparable structure: a subject is that which is aware of experience; awareness is the subject’s lived access to experience; experience is that of which the subject can be aware. They are not reducible in isolation; each implies the others in any act of experiencing.

## Philosophical Translation (of formal claim)
“Subject,” “awareness,” and “experience” are three faces of the same fact: if any is present, all are present; you can’t peel them apart without losing what they mean.

## Philosophical Justification
The terms co-define each other: without awareness there is no subject in the usual sense; without experience there is nothing to be aware of; without a subject, awareness/experience are empty. Attempts to deny one while asserting another collapse into contradiction.

## Explanation (informal)
Makes explicit the triad at the ground of phenomenology: the “someone,” the “being‑aware,” and the “what” are a single act, not separable modules. This sets the base for later structure (fields, models, reports).

## Derivation (Philosophical)
- From [[S-DF-subject]], [[S-DF-awareness]], [[S-DF-experience]]: each definition invokes the others; consistency requires treating them as a unified structure.
- Performative consistency: doubting any one still presupposes experiencing (cf. [[S-FT-immediate-datum]]), which re‑introduces the triad.

## Derivation (Formal/Logical/Mathematical)
```text
∀e: Experience(e) ⇒ ∃s: Subject(s) ∧ Awareness(s,e)
∀s: Subject(s) ⇒ ∃e: Experience(e) ∧ Awareness(s,e)
```
mutual implication captures inseparability; no independent projection is admissible.

## Clarifications / Further Context
- This is ontological, not a claim about neural implementation.
- Avoids reifying “awareness” as substance; keeps it relational.
- Serves as parent for capacity/instance clarifications and for denying “pure content” or “bare subject” edge cases.
