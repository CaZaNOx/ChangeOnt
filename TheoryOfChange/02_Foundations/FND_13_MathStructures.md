# FND_ClosureAlgebra

## 1. Philosophical Rationale

Breathing‐closure zones (BCZs) capture local coherence, but unlike classical open‐sets they do **not** always behave under intersection or union.  To understand entropy and stability correctly, we must **explicitly** characterize how BCZs combine—and where pure Change pauses—so we can introduce only the minimal, justified repairs.

---

## 2. Definitions

Let $\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(x)$ and $\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(y)$ be two closure zones under the same parameters $(\delta_o,D)$.

1.  **Union:**
	$$
        \mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(x)\;\cup\;\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(y)
        \;=\;\{\,z \mid z\in\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(x)\text{ or }z\in\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(y)\}.
    $$

2.  **Intersection (naïve):**
    $$
        \mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(x)\;\cap\;\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(y)
        \;=\;\{\,z \mid z\in\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(x)\text{ and }z\in\mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(y)\}.
    $$

---

## 3. Algebraic Properties

| Property                 | Formal Statement                                                                | Status in Pure Change Ontology                                                                    |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Idempotence**          | $\mathrm{BCZ}_D(\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)) = \mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)$                         | **Holds approximately**: repeated closure stabilizes after one application if memory persists.    |
| **Monotonicity**         | If $x \to y$ within $D$, then $\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)\subseteq\mathrm{BCZ}_D(y)$?    | **Partially**: true when contrast chains preserve returnability, but can fail under drift.        |
| **Union‐Closure**        | $\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)\cup\mathrm{BCZ}_D(y)$ is itself a BCZ (for some base point)? | **Holds**: union of two coherent zones may form a larger coherent zone under extended D.          |
| **Intersection‐Closure** | $\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)\cap\mathrm{BCZ}_D(y)$ is a BCZ of some $z$?                  | **Fails generally**: common intersection need not return to a single anchor without extra memory. |

---

## 4. Minimal Repair via Memory Persistence

To recover intersection‐closure, we introduce a **memory‐persistence parameter** $M$ (how long past returns are remembered):

- **Modified Intersection:**  
$$
    \mathrm{BCZ}_{D,M}(x)\cap\mathrm{BCZ}_{D,M}(y)
    = \mathrm{BCZ}_{D}(z)
$$
  where $z$ is any pattern in the naïve intersection such that both return‐paths remain within memory window $M$.

- **Justification:**  
  This adds **no new primitive**—memory persistence is itself a pattern of contrasts (long‐term return sequences).

---

## 5. Illustrative Example

Consider a tiny flux graph with nodes A, B, C, D:

- A ↔ B (mutual 2‐step return)  
- B ↔ C  
- C ↔ D  

With $D=2$:

- $\mathrm{BCZ}_2(A)=\{A,B\}$  
- $\mathrm{BCZ}_2(B)=\{A,B,C\}$  
- **Naïve intersection:** $\{A,B\}\cap\{A,B,C\}=\{A,B\}$ which is $\mathrm{BCZ}_2(A)$ → **happens**.  
- **Counterexample:** If B↔C returns take 3 steps, then $\{B,C\}\cap\{C,D\}=\{C\}$, but C alone may not return within D→ **fails** without memory M≥3.

---

## 6. Implications for Metrics

- **Entropy (Σ):** Must count only those overlapping shells whose intersections satisfy memory persistence M.  
- **Stability (Eₛ):** Variance of lifetimes should include penalties for intersections that fall outside of $M$.  

By formalizing BCZ algebra this way, we ensure **all** later metrics rest on a **consistent**, fully justified closure framework.

# FND_LimitConvergence

## 1. Philosophical Rationale

Our metrics—breathing dimension **dim_B**, entropy **Σ**, and stability energy **Eₛ**—are defined as limits as $D\to\infty$.  To ensure they are well-posed (not merely formal), we need conditions under which these sequences converge.  Philosophically, continuity of change and bounded local complexity suggest such convergence is natural in realistic fluxes.

---

## 2. Convergence of $\dim_B(x)$

### 2.1. Setup

- Recall $N_D(x)$ = maximum size of an independent basis of return-paths of length ≤ D.  
- We define
$$
    \dim_B(x)
    := \lim_{D\to\infty} \frac{\log N_D(x)}{\log D}.
$$

### 2.2. Submultiplicative Growth

- **Claim:** In most flux models, $N_{D_1+D_2}(x) \le N_{D_1}(x)\,N_{D_2}(x)$.  
- **Reasoning:** Any independent path of length ≤ $D_1+D_2$ can be split at an intermediate return, yielding two segments of lengths ≤ $D_1$ and ≤ $D_2$; independence limits their product.

### 2.3. Application of Fekete’s Lemma

- **Fekete’s Lemma:** For any submultiplicative sequence $a_D$, the limit $\lim_{D\to\infty}\frac{\log a_D}{D}$ exists and equals $\inf_{D}\frac{\log a_D}{D}$.  
- **Adaptation:** Setting $a_D = N_D(x)$ and re-indexing by $D=\exp(t)$ yields existence of $\dim_B(x)$ as a lim inf = lim sup.

---

## 3. Convergence of $\Sigma(x)$

### 3.1. Setup

- $\Sigma_D(x) = -\sum_{y\in\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)} p_{x,D}(y)\,\log p_{x,D}(y)$.  
- We require $\lim_{D\to\infty}\Sigma_D(x)$ exists.

### 3.2. Boundedness and Monotonicity

- **Bounded Above:** Maximum entropy over a set of size $M_D=|\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)|$ is $\log M_D$, which grows at most like $\log N_D(x)$.  
- **Near-Monotonicity:** As $D$ increases, $\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)$ expands or stabilizes, and the distribution $p_{x,D}$ refines—so $\Sigma_D(x)$ is roughly non-decreasing.

### 3.3. Existence of Limit

- A bounded, non-decreasing sequence has a limit (possibly infinite).  
- Under realistic flux models where BCZ growth is polynomial, $\Sigma(x)<\infty$.

---

## 4. Convergence of $Eₛ(x)$

### 4.1. Setup

- $Eₛ_D(x) = \mathrm{Var}(\{\tau(p): p\in\mathcal{P}^r_D(x)\})$.  
- We require $\lim_{D\to\infty}Eₛ_D(x)$.

### 4.2. Bounded Variance

- **Bounded Moments:** If path‐length distributions have uniformly bounded second moments (plausible in non-explosive fluxes), then $Eₛ_D(x)$ remains bounded.

### 4.3. Cauchy Criterion

- To prove convergence, show $|Eₛ_{D_2}(x)-Eₛ_{D_1}(x)|\to 0$ as $D_1,D_2\to\infty$.  
- In practice, additional **mixing** or **ergodicity** assumptions on the flux ensure path‐length distributions stabilize.

---

## 5. Summary of Conditions

1. **Submultiplicative Independence:** $N_{D_1+D_2}(x)\le N_{D_1}(x)\,N_{D_2}(x)$.  
2. **Bounded Growth of BCZ:** $|\mathrm{BCZ}_D(x)|$ grows at most polynomially in $D$.  
3. **Non-decreasing Entropy:** $\Sigma_D(x)$ is bounded and roughly monotonic.  
4. **Bounded Path‐Length Variance:** Second moments of return‐path lengths remain finite.  

Under these mild, realistic assumptions, our three foundational metrics converge, cementing their mathematical rigor without introducing new hidden axioms.

# FND_EmpiricalCalibration

## 1. Philosophical Rationale

To move from abstract definitions to applied insights, we must show **how to choose** observer parameters $(\delta_o, D_o)$ and **validate** metrics on real or simulated flux data.  Empirical calibration ensures our theory isn’t merely conceptual but also operational.

---

## 2. Choosing $\delta_o$ (Differentiation Threshold)

1. **Signal‐to‐Noise Analysis**  
   - Collect a sample of raw differentiation magnitudes $\{\Delta(x,y)\}$.  
   - Plot their distribution; identify a **noise floor** (e.g., low‐amplitude fluctuations).  
   - Set $\delta_o$ above this floor (e.g., at the 95th percentile of noise magnitudes) to ensure **salient** contrasts only.

2. **Stability Sensitivity Test**  
   - Vary $\delta_o$ across a plausible range.  
   - Compute **dim_B, Σ, Eₛ** at each setting.  
   - Identify a plateau region where metrics are **robust**—choose $\delta_o$ within that plateau for stability.

---

## 3. Choosing $D_o$ (Context Depth)

1. **Memory‐Decay Observation**  
   - Measure how often patterns return to themselves after $k$ contrasts.  
   - Plot return‐frequency vs. $k$; find the scale $D_o$ where return‐frequency drops below a threshold (e.g., 5%).

2. **Convergence Horizon**  
   - Increase $D_o$ incrementally, computing BCZ sizes and metrics.  
   - Locate the **elbow** in the curve where additional depth yields diminishing changes—select $D_o$ at that elbow.

---

## 4. Worked Example on Simulated Flux

- **Validation:**  
  - Compute **dim_B, Σ, Eₛ** at $(0.15, 5)$, $(0.2,8)$, $(0.25,12)$.

---

## 5. Integrating Calibration into Workflow

1. **Data Collection:**  Gather Δ‐weights and return‐histories from simulation or real‐world traces.

With empirical calibration, Change Ontology’s constructs become **actionable**—ready for modeling, prediction, and deeper theoretical exploration.

# FND_ContrastDetection

## 1. Philosophical Motivation

Detecting a genuine contrast (x → y) requires distinguishing **salient, local** differentiations from minor fluctuations or distant correlations.  We model this with two observer‐parameters:

- **δₒ (Magnitude Threshold):** Minimum ontic “strength” of differentiation to count as real.  
- **Dₒ (Context Depth):** How many contrast‐steps into the flux the observer can track before uncertainty erodes.

These ensure that x → y captures an **unambiguous**, **finite**, **semantically relevant** transition in the continuous flux.

---

## 2. Formal Criterion

$$
x \;\to\; y
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\bigl(\Delta(x,y)\ge\delta_o\bigr)
\;\wedge\;
\bigl(y\in N_{\delta_o,D_o}^{+}(x)\bigr),
$$
where $N_{\delta_o,D_o}^{+}(x)$ is the set of patterns reachable from $x$ within $D_o$ steps using only edges of weight $\ge\delta_o$.

---

## 3. Contrast‐Detection Algorithm

```python
def detect_contrast(x, y, delta_o, D_o, flux_graph):
    """
    Determine if x → y under observer parameters.
    
    Parameters:
    - x, y: pattern identifiers
    - delta_o: differentiation magnitude threshold
    - D_o: context depth limit
    - flux_graph: directed graph with edge weights Δ(x,y)
    
    Returns:
    - True if x → y (salient, local contrast), else False.
    """
    # 1. Magnitude check
    if flux_graph.weight(x, y) < delta_o:
        return False
    
    # 2. Breadth-first search up to D_o steps
    frontier = {x}
    visited = set()
    for _ in range(D_o):
        next_frontier = set()
        for node in frontier:
            for succ in flux_graph.successors(node):
                if flux_graph.weight(node, succ) >= delta_o and succ not in visited:
                    next_frontier.add(succ)
        visited |= frontier
        frontier = next_frontier
        if y in frontier:
            return True
    return False


---

4. Measuring Context Depth

Dₒ = 10 vs. Dₒ = 100

Dₒ = 10: The observer tracks up to 10 successive contrasts from x.  Patterns that return or emerge within 10 steps are considered “local.”  Beyond that, they’re treated as too distant or uncertain.

Dₒ = 100: A much broader horizon—allows detection of long, winding chains of change.  More remote patterns can still count as contrasts, expanding the local neighborhood.



Implications:

A small Dₒ yields tight, highly reliable contrast detection—ideal for noisy or fast‐changing environments.

A large Dₒ captures deeper structural relations but risks conflating unrelated distant events.


Choosing Dₒ balances fidelity against scope—a core trade‐off in Change Ontology’s locality formalism.


---

5. Next Steps

Integrate this algorithm into FND_ContrastRelation.md to ground x → y.

Use variable Dₒ settings in simulations to study how locality bounds shape breathing closures and metrics.```



# MAT_ChoicePrinciple

This file introduces and contextualizes the “Choice‐for‐Contrasts” principle in Change Ontology, showing how and when to include or exclude it, and how classical Axiom of Choice analogues map into our framework.

---

## 1. Choice‐for‐Contrasts: Definition

- **Contrast‐Classes:** Equivalence classes of contrast‐chains that lead into the same breathing‐closure zone or share initial segments.  
- **Choice‐for‐Contrasts (C):**  
  > For every non‐empty family $\mathcal{F}$ of contrast‐classes, there exists a function  
  > $\chi: \mathcal{F}\to \bigcup\mathcal{F}$  
  > such that $\chi(F)\in F$ for each $F\in\mathcal{F}$.

---

## 2. Ontic Infinity vs. Epistemic Horizon

1. **Ontic Flux Infinity:**  
   - Reality supports arbitrarily long chains of Δ‐events.  

1. **Epistemic Horizon $M$:**  
   - Any observer can track at most $M$ successive contrasts.  

3. **Modeling Choice:**  
   - Base algebra uses $\mathcal{C}_M$.  
   - “Choice‐for‐Contrasts” governs selections **among** those finite classes or, in the limit layer, among infinite classes.

---

## 3. Two Flavors of Change‐Math

| Version        | Axiom Used                    | Use Cases                                           |
|----------------|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| **ZF‐Style**   | No Choice (constructive only) | Algorithmic theorems, simulations, effective models |
| **ZFC‐Style**  | Full Choice                   | Meta‐theory, global existence proofs                |

---

## 4. Choice‐Degree Parameter $\chi$

- $\chi = 0$: No Choice; only explicit, constructive selections allowed.  
- $\chi = 1$: Countable Choice (Choice among countable families).  
- $\chi = \infty$: Full Choice (all families).

This parameter tunes the **strength** of Choice used in proofs.

---

## 5. Annotating Theorems

- Tag purely constructive results with `[Constructive]`.  
- Tag results needing Choice with `[Requires Choice]`.  

Example:
```markdown
**Theorem 5.1 (Existence of Maximal BCZ‐Basis) [Requires Choice]**

---

6. When to Exclude Choice

Finite or computably enumerable contrast‐graphs.

Any context requiring an effective algorithm for chain selection.


7. When to Include Choice

Proving global structural theorems about infinite‐depth recursion.

Constructing non‐constructive objects (e.g. well‐orders, maximal ideals).



---

8. Philosophical Rationale

Real Observers have finite memory—no Choice reflects their limits.

Ideal Theorists explore the full breadth of Change‐Ontology—with Choice they attain maximal generality.```

# MAT_ClassicalDerivations

This file shows how the full machinery of classical mathematics—ℕ, ℤ, ℚ, ℝ, metric spaces, topology, and calculus—emerges as **special cases** or **limits** of Change-Ontology’s contrast-chain algebra.

---

## 1. Peano Arithmetic from a Single Loop

1. **Choose** a distinguished minimal loop contrast $\ell\in\Delta$ (an ε₀–cycle).  
2. **Define** natural numbers as repeated concatenations:
$$
     0 \;=\; []
     ,\quad
     1 \;=\; [\ell]
     ,\quad
     n \;=\;\underbrace{\ell\oplus\ell\oplus\cdots\oplus\ell}_{n\text{ times}}.
$$
3. **Successor:** $S(n)=n\oplus\ell$.  
4. **Peano Axioms**  
   - $0\neq S(n)$.  
   - $S$ is injective: $S(m)=S(n)\implies m=n$.  
   - Induction follows because any nonempty set of chains closed under $\oplus\ell$ must contain 0.

*Thus $(\mathbb{N},0,S)$ is recovered constructively.*

---

## 2. Integers and Commutative Sub-Ring

1. **Integers $\mathbb{Z}$:**  
   - Allow inverses of $\ell$: $-\ell$.  
   - Every $n\in\mathbb{N}$ has $-n = -([\,\ell\oplus\cdots\oplus\ell\,])$.  
   - Addition matches $\oplus$; associativity, identity, and inverses hold.  
1. **Commutative Sub-Ring:**  
   - Restrict to the subalgebra generated by $\ell$.  
   - Here $\ell\oplus\ell = \ell\oplus\ell$ and order is irrelevant → commutative ring isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}$.

---

## 3. Rationals as Fractions of Chains

1. **Fractional Chains:**  
   - Formal pairs $(a,b)$ with $a,b\in\mathcal{C}_M$, $b\neq 0$.  
   - Interpret $(a,b)$ as “$a$ divided by $b$.”  
1. **Equivalence:**  
   $(a,b)\sim(c,d)$ if $a\otimes d = c\otimes b$ under fusion-overlap.  
2. **Operations:**  
   - Addition: $\frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a\otimes d \oplus c\otimes b}{b\otimes d}$.  
   - Multiplication: $\frac{a}{b}\times\frac{c}{d} = \frac{a\otimes c}{b\otimes d}$.  
1. **Recovery of $\mathbb{Q}$** once $\ell$-chains commute.

---

## 4. Real Numbers via Completion

1. **Infinite Horizon:** Let $M\to\infty$, allowing arbitrarily long chains.  
2. **Contrast-Chain Embedding:** Map each real $x\in\mathbb{R}$ to a (possibly infinite) chain with contrasts summing to $x$.  
3. **Cauchy Chains:** A sequence of finite chains $(a_k)$ is **Cauchy** if for every “small contrast” threshold $\varepsilon$, beyond some $K$, all $a_j\oplus(-a_i)$ lie within BCZ of size $\varepsilon$.  
4. **Completion:** Identify equivalence classes of Cauchy chains → recover $\mathbb{R}$.  
5. **Addition/Multiplication:** Extend $\oplus,\otimes$ by continuity.

---

## 5. Metric & Topology

1. **Quasi-Metric:**  
$$
     d(a,b) = \min\{\,n\mid \exists\text{ chain of length }n\text{ from }a\text{ to }b\}.
$$
2. **Open Sets:**  
   - A BCZ neighborhood $N_D(a)$ = all chains reachable within contrast-depth $D$.  
   - As $D\to\infty$, these generate a topology matching classical ε–δ.  
1. **Continuity:**  
   A function $f$ between contrast-spaces is continuous if preimages of BCZs are BCZs at some (possibly different) depth.

---

## 6. Calculus of Change

1. **Derivative:**  
$$
     f'(a) = \lim_{[\Delta]\to 0}
     \frac{f(a\oplus[\Delta]) \oplus (-f(a))}{[\Delta]},
$$
   where $[\Delta]$ is an infinitesimal contrast.  
2. **Integral:**  
   Sum of contrast-returns:
$$
     \int_a^b f = \lim_{D\to\infty}

$$
   where $\{a_k\}$ partitions the contrast-chain from $a$ to $b$.  
3. **Differential Equations:**  
   Recursive BCZ definitions of $x_{t+1} = x_t \oplus [F(x_t)]$ emulate $\dot x = F(x)$.

## 7. Contextual Choice of Math

- **Finite $M$, non-commutative $\oplus$:** Non-classical dynamics.  
- **$M\to\infty$, single commuting loop:** Recovers all of classical analysis.  
- **Intermediate settings:** Yield hybrid “dynamic-topological” frameworks best suited to specific domains.

# MAT_GroupAxioms

This file proves that, for any observer with horizon $M$, the set of all contrast-chains of length $\le M$, under concatenation $\oplus$, forms a (non-commutative) group‐like structure. “Finiteness” is an **observer-modeling** choice, not a hidden metaphysical axiom.

---

## 1. Observer Horizon and Ontic vs. Epistemic Infinity

- **Ontic Flux Infinity:** In reality, there is an unbroken continuum of Δ-events; chains can be arbitrarily long.  
- **Epistemic Horizon** $M$: Any observer (or formal model) can record at most $M$ successive Δ-events.  
- **Modeling Convention:** We work on  
$$
    \mathcal{C}_M = \{\,a=[Δ_1,\dots,Δ_n]\mid 0 \le n \le M\}.
$$
  Chains of length >$M$ are handled via the separate “limit” layer (e.g. $\limdim$), not here.

---

## 2. Definitions

1. **Contrast-chain**: a finite list  
$$
     a = [Δ_1,Δ_2,\dots,Δ_n],\quad 0\le n\le M.
$$
2. **Addition** $\oplus$: concatenation  
$$
     [Δ_1,\dots,Δ_n]\;\oplus\;[Δ'_1,\dots,Δ'_m]
     = [Δ_1,\dots,Δ_n,\,Δ'_1,\dots,Δ'_m],
     \quad n+m\le M.
$$
3. **Zero** $0$: the empty chain $[]$.  
4. **Inverse** $-a$: if  
$$
     a=[Δ_1,\dots,Δ_n],
$$
   then  
$$
     -a = [Δ_n^{-1},\,Δ_{n-1}^{-1},\dots,Δ_1^{-1}],
$$
   provided each $Δ_i^{-1}$ exists and $n\le M$.

---

## 3. Group-Axiom Proofs

### 3.1 Closure

**Claim.** For all $a,b\in\mathcal C_M$, $a\oplus b\in\mathcal C_M$.

**Proof.**  
- Let $a$ have length $n\le M$ and $b$ length $m\le M$.  
- By definition, $a\oplus b$ has length $n+m$.  
- We require $n+m\le M$ by construction (or model memory loss beyond $M$).  
- Hence $a\oplus b$ is a valid chain of length $\le M$. ∎

### 3.2 Associativity

**Claim.** $(a\oplus b)\oplus c = a\oplus(b\oplus c)$.

**Proof.**  
Write  
$$
  a=[Δ^a_1,\dots,Δ^a_n],\;
  b=[Δ^b_1,\dots,Δ^b_m],\;
  c=[Δ^c_1,\dots,Δ^c_p].
$$
Then both sides expand to  
$[Δ^a_1,\dots,Δ^a_n,Δ^b_1,\dots,Δ^b_m,Δ^c_1,\dots,Δ^c_p]$. ∎

### 3.3 Identity Element

**Claim.** $0\oplus a = a\oplus 0 = a$.

**Proof.**  
- $0\oplus a = []\oplus[\dots] = a$.  
- $a\oplus 0 = [\dots]\oplus[] = a$. ∎

### 3.4 Inverses

**Claim.** If $-a$ exists in $\mathcal C_M$, then $a\oplus(-a)=0$ and $(-a)\oplus a=0$.

**Proof.**  
$$
  a\oplus(-a)
  = [Δ_1,\dots,Δ_n,\,Δ_n^{-1},\dots,Δ_1^{-1}]
  \;\longrightarrow\; [] = 0,
$$
by cancellation of each $Δ_i$ with $Δ_i^{-1}$. ∎

### 3.5 Non-Commutativity

**Claim.** In general $a\oplus b \neq b\oplus a$.

**Proof.**  
Order matters. E.g.  
$$
  [Δ_A]\oplus[Δ_B] = [Δ_A,Δ_B]\neq[Δ_B,Δ_A].
$$ 

---

# MAT_RingExtensions

This file extends the group‐like algebra of contrast-chains into a (near-)ring by introducing a multiplication operator $\otimes$.  We prove which distributive laws hold, where commutativity fails, and how this shapes the algebraic landscape of Change Ontology.

---

## 1. Multiplication Definition

Choose one of two natural interpretations of $\otimes$:

1. **Concatenation‐Interleaving**  
$$
     a\otimes b \;=\; \bigl\{\,a\oplus b,\;b\oplus a\,\bigr\}
$$
   (the set of both possible concatenations)—useful for modeling **incoherent overlaps**.

2. **Fusion‐Overlap**  
$$
     a\otimes b \;=\; \bigl[a_1,\dots,a_n,\;b_1,\dots,b_m\bigr]
     \cap_{D,M}
     \bigl[b_1,\dots,b_m,\;a_1,\dots,a_n\bigr]
$$
   where $\cap_{D,M}$ is the observer-horizon intersection (only sequences stable within $(D,M)$ memory).

_For clarity, we will focus on_ **Fusion-Overlap**.

---

## 2. Distributive Laws

We ask whether
$$
  a\otimes(b\oplus c) \;\stackrel{?}{=}\; (a\otimes b)\oplus(a\otimes c),
  \quad
  (b\oplus c)\otimes a \;\stackrel{?}{=}\;(b\otimes a)\oplus(c\otimes a).
$$

### 2.1 Left-Distributivity

**Claim.**  Left-distributivity may hold under strict contrast-alignment.

**Proof Sketch.**  
- If $b$ and $c$ share the same initial contrasts up to depth $D$, then the fusion‐overlap with $a$ yields:
$$
    a\otimes(b\oplus c)
    = (a\oplus b)\cap(a\oplus c)
    = (a\cap a)\oplus(b\cap c)
    = a\oplus(b\cap c).
$$
- Meanwhile,
$$
    (a\otimes b)\oplus(a\otimes c)
    = \bigl[(a\oplus b)\cap(a\oplus c)\bigr],
$$
  which matches under the same alignment.  
- **Limitation:** If $b$ and $c$ diverge in their first contrasts, this fails. ∎

### 2.2 Right-Distributivity

**Claim.**  Right-distributivity generally **fails** due to non-commutativity of $\oplus$.

**Counterexample.**  
Let
$$
  a=[X],\;
  b=[A],\;
  c=[B].
$$
Then
$$
  (b\oplus c)\otimes a
  = ([A,B]\cap[A,B])\oplus[X]
  = [A,B,X],
$$
but
$$
  (b\otimes a)\oplus(c\otimes a)
  = [A,X]\oplus[B,X]
  = [A,X,B,X],
$$
which is not the same. ∎

---

## 3. Commutativity in the Ring

**Claim.**  $\otimes$ is **non-commutative** whenever $\oplus$ is.

**Proof Sketch.**  
By construction, fusion-overlap depends on the order of concatenation:
$$
  a\otimes b
  = (a\oplus b)\cap(b\oplus a),
$$
which is symmetric in the set sense, but the result inside depends on $\oplus$.  In general,
$$
  a\otimes b \;\neq\; b\otimes a
$$
because the overlap zones differ when $a$ and $b$ swap. ∎

---

## 4. Near-Ring Summary

- **Additive Structure** $(\mathcal C_M,\oplus,0)$: non-commutative near-group.  
- **Multiplicative Structure** $(\mathcal C_M,\otimes)$:  
  - **Left-distributive** under contrast-alignment.  
  - **Right-distributivity fails** in general.  
  - **Non-commutative**, reflecting temporal ordering and overlap sensitivity.

Thus, $(\mathcal C_M,\oplus,\otimes)$ forms a **non-commutative near-ring** perfectly aligned with Change Ontology’s primitives.

---

## 5. Implications for Dynamics

- **Asymmetry** of $\otimes$ models how **initial conditions** shape emergent structures differently than **final conditions**.  
- **One-sided distributivity** captures why **feeding** a pattern into a change‐system differs from **splitting** that pattern.  
- This algebraic asymmetry underlies **spiral bifurcations**, **meta-knot birth**, and **irreversible transformations** in real systems.
