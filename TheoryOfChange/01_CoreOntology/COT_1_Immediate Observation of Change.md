# 1_Immediate_Observation_of_Change

## 1.1 The Necessity of Grounding in Experience

A sound ontology shouldn't begin from abstract speculation. Instead it should originate from direct accessible experiences and reflections of the reader.

As a result the ontology has to be universally accessible without smuggeling in controversial assumptions and should be verifiable by direct observation and reasoning performed by the reader.

This serves to prevent arbitrary starting points.


Thus, we ask:

> *What is the most undeniable feature of reality?*

---

## 1.2 The Ubiquity of Change

Change is not a theoretical construct   inferred at later stages of reasoning.
Instead it is present in every act of perception or thought and can be discovered at every moment of relational or temporal unfolding.


Whenever we experience anything —  
whether sensation, thought, emotion, or external observation —  
we experience **difference**:

- A fluctuation in quality,
- A shift in position,
- A transformation of state.

Without change:

- No perception could occur,
- No recognition could arise,
- No memory could form,
- No action could unfold.

---

## 1.3 Change Compared to Other Proposed Foundations

Several classical candidates for ontological primacy —  
such as "substance," "being," "mind," or "matter" —  
lack the same immediacy and undeniability as change.

For example:

- **Substance**: inferred stability behind phenomena; not directly observed.
- **Being**: an abstraction about presence; does not specify structure or dynamics.
- **Mind**: requires a distinction between subject and object,  
which itself presupposes change.
- **Matter**: a concept constructed from repeated regularities across change;  
not immediately encountered apart from change.

In contrast:

- **Change** is encountered without mediation or inference.
- It requires no theory to recognize.
- It permeates all perceptual and cognitive fields.

Thus:

> **Change stands as the minimal, immediate datum upon which a sound ontology may be built.**

## 1.4 Structural Saturation of Change

Change is not a localized phenomenon,  
confined to specific instances.

It is **structurally saturated** across every level of experience:

- Sensory:  
  Light, sound, touch — all registered as variations over time.
- Cognitive:  
  Thought processes unfold through differentiation.
- Emotional:  
  Affective states shift, evolve, recur, or dissipate.
- Environmental:  
  External phenomena are registered through successive contrast and transition.

Thus:

- No domain of experience escapes change.
- No cognitive access exists outside dynamic transformation.

Even the concept of a "stable object" —  
such as a mountain or an electron —  
is meaningful only against a background of ongoing change:

- The mountain persists because it persists **through** erosion, weather, and geological shifts.
- The electron maintains identity through dynamic internal and external interactions.

---

## 1.5 Resistance to Change Primacy: Possible Objections

Some may argue:

- That stability, not change, is primary,
- Or that change presupposes stable entities undergoing modification.

However:

- Stability itself is recognized only through the persistence of patterns **across transformations**.
- A stable entity is identified by its **resistance to change**,  
which itself presupposes a field of ongoing transformation  
against which such resistance becomes meaningful.

Thus:

- The recognition of stability already presupposes the primacy of change,
- Not the other way around.

---

## 1.6 Consequences of This Observation

Given that change:

- Is immediately accessible,
- Pervades all fields of experience,
- Requires no mediation or inference,
- Structures the recognition of stability itself,

it follows that:

- **Change is the minimal and necessary ground upon which to build ontology**.

This does not deny the reality of stability, being, or mind.  
Rather, it situates them properly:

- As emergent structures,
- Within continuous transformation,
- Not as independent metaphysical primitives.

→ Change is a prerequisite for truth, falsity, and any kind of formal system.

### 1.2 The Collapse of the Dualist Claim

**Argument:**  
- Any concept of “being” must be distinct to be intelligible.  
- Distinction implies difference, which implies change.  
- Therefore, being cannot be understood without change already active.

**Further:**  
- An unchanging substrate is unobservable, untestable, and epistemically indistinguishable from non-existence.  
- Any stability we experience is the result of **recursive regularity in change**, not proof of a metaphysical “being.”
