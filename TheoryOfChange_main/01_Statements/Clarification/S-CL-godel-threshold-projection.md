# Treat “sudden” change as thresholded projection of a Gödel-bounded flow

AI_13 explains how Gödel-hole language fits the continuity debate. Local stasis is either a modeling choice (discrete ticks, jump processes, non-Lipschitz departures) or a projection: hidden variables drift until thresholds fire, so the observable looks abrupt while the underlying metaflow never halts. Gödel holes mark those projection boundaries—regions where your cut or instrumentation cannot see the latent change that seeds the “sudden” event. To stay honest, declare which dynamics you permit (dense order, fast–slow systems, stochastic jumps) and demonstrate at least one concrete case (decision onset, phase transition, Gödel-pressure example) showing how the model explains apparent jumps without smuggling continuity. Owning the axioms (A0–A2) plus the Gödel-boundary interpretation turns “always-on flux” from rhetoric into a testable modeling stance.
